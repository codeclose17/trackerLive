CHAPTER 01
Foundations

What ADHD is — and how it's defined.

⚡ 30-second version
  • ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder of self-regulation — attention, impulse, and activity — not a character flaw.
  • Diagnosis is clinical (DSM-5-TR / ICD-11): a pattern of symptoms present early, across settings, causing real impairment.
  • Three presentations: inattentive, hyperactive-impulsive, and combined.

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder is a neurodevelopmental condition characterised by a persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that is more frequent and severe than expected for a person's developmental level, and that interferes with functioning. The name is a historical misnomer: it is less a deficit of attention than a dysregulation of attention and self-control — the ability to deploy attention where and when it's needed.

The DSM-5-TR criteria (the diagnostic backbone)

The American Psychiatric Association's DSM-5-TR defines ADHD across two symptom domains. A person needs ≥6 symptoms (≥5 for those 17+) from a domain, persisting ≥6 months.

Inattention (9 symptoms)

Careless mistakes · trouble sustaining attention · not seeming to listen · failing to finish tasks · disorganisation · avoiding sustained mental effort · losing things · easy distractibility · forgetfulness in daily activities.

Hyperactivity-Impulsivity (9)

Fidgeting · leaving seat · restlessness ("driven by a motor") · trouble being quiet · talking excessively · blurting answers · trouble waiting turn · interrupting · being "on the go".

The five gatekeeping rules

  1. Onset: several symptoms present before age 12.
  2. Pervasiveness: symptoms in ≥2 settings (e.g. home and work/school).
  3. Impairment: clear evidence symptoms interfere with functioning or quality of life.
  4. Not better explained by another mental disorder.
  5. Specify severity (mild / moderate / severe) and presentation.

The three presentations

PresentationDominant featuresNotes
Predominantly InattentiveDistractibility, forgetfulness, disorganisation, "spacey"Historically under-diagnosed; more common in girls/women; formerly "ADD".

Presentations are not fixed subtypes — they can shift across a lifetime as hyperactivity often internalises into restlessness with age.

🐿 ICD-11 note

The WHO's ICD-11 uses the near-equivalent term "Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder" (6A05) with comparable domains. The DSM and ICD have converged substantially, though ICD-11 places less emphasis on rigid symptom counts and more on the overall clinical pattern.

How common it is — and who gets diagnosed.

⚡ 30-second version
  • ~5–7% of children and ~2.5% of adults worldwide — a genuinely common condition.
  • Diagnosed far more in boys, but the true sex gap is much smaller than the diagnosis gap.
  • It persists: roughly half to two-thirds of childhood cases carry meaningful symptoms into adulthood.

Prevalence estimates have been remarkably stable across cultures once diagnostic method is held constant — arguing against the "modern invention" narrative. Apparent national differences are driven mostly by diagnostic thresholds and awareness, not real biology. The rise in diagnoses over recent decades reflects better recognition (especially of inattentive presentations, adults, and girls) rather than an epidemic.

The diagnosis gap vs the prevalence gap

Boys are diagnosed 2–3× more often, but community screening studies find the real ratio closer to ~1.5–2:1. The difference is a referral and recognition bias: boys more often show disruptive, externalising hyperactivity that gets flagged; girls more often show quiet inattention that gets missed or mislabelled as anxiety, daydreaming, or "not trying".

The persistence question

Longitudinal cohorts (e.g. the Multimodal Treatment Study, MTA) show that while the full childhood syndrome remits in a substantial fraction, most retain impairing residual symptoms — particularly inattention and executive dysfunction — well into adulthood. Recent work also describes a fluctuating course: symptoms can wax and wane with life demands rather than following a single trajectory.

CHAPTER 03
Foundations

The genetics: one of psychiatry's most heritable traits.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Twin studies put heritability at ~74–80% — comparable to height.
  • It's polygenic: hundreds to thousands of common variants each add a tiny nudge, plus rarer large-effect variants.
  • Genes converge on dopamine, neurodevelopment, and synaptic signalling.

ADHD is strongly familial. Twin and adoption studies converge on a heritability of roughly 74–80%, meaning most of the population variation in ADHD traits is explained by genetic differences. First-degree relatives of someone with ADHD are several times more likely to have it themselves. This does not make it "genetically determined" — genes set a liability that environment modulates.

Approximate heritability (twin-study estimate)~74%

Polygenic architecture

ADHD is not a single-gene condition. The 2019 (and expanded 2023) genome-wide association studies by Demontis, Børglum and colleagues identified a dozen and then 27+ genome-wide significant loci, with a polygenic score built from thousands of common variants. On top of this common-variant load sit rarer copy-number variants (CNVs) and de novo mutations with larger individual effects. Genetic risk overlaps substantially with autism, depression, and other conditions — evidence of shared neurodevelopmental pathways.

Each of these individually explains only a sliver of risk. Their value is conceptual: they point unmistakably at dopamine signalling and synaptic/neurodevelopmental machinery.

Gene × environment

Genetic liability interacts with prenatal and perinatal factors that raise risk: maternal smoking and alcohol, extreme prematurity, very low birth weight, prenatal stress, and lead exposure. These are risk modifiers, not standalone causes — and their statistical association is partly confounded by shared genetics (a parent with ADHD is more likely to smoke and to pass on risk alleles).

CHAPTER 04
The physical brain

The hardware: structure, maturation, and wiring.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Differences are subtle, statistical, and average-level — you cannot diagnose ADHD from a scan.
  • The ADHD cortex matures on a ~2–3 year delay — "delayed, not deviant."
  • Key nodes: prefrontal cortex, striatum, cerebellum, and the tracts connecting them.

The largest structural study, ENIGMA-ADHD (Hoogman et al., 2017; 3,200+ participants), found slightly smaller volumes in several subcortical structures — most consistently the amygdala, accumbens, caudate, putamen, and hippocampus, plus reduced total brain and cortical surface area. Effect sizes were small (Cohen's d ~0.1–0.2) and largest in children, shrinking with age — consistent with a maturational lag rather than permanent damage.

The regions and what they do

Prefrontal cortex (PFC)

The executive "CEO" — planning, working memory, inhibition, self-monitoring. Under-activated in ADHD (hypofrontality), especially dorsolateral and ventrolateral PFC.

Striatum / basal ganglia

Caudate, putamen, nucleus accumbens — the reward and go/no-go gate. Central to motivation, habit, and delay of gratification.

Cerebellum

Beyond motor control, it times and coordinates cognition. Reduced vermis volume links to timing deficits and "time blindness."

Anterior cingulate cortex

Error detection, conflict monitoring, effort allocation. Hypoactive during attention tasks in ADHD.

Frontostriatal & frontoparietal tracts

The white-matter "cables." Diffusion MRI shows lower fractional anisotropy — noisier, higher-latency signalling.

Amygdala

Emotional salience. Its altered coupling with PFC underlies the emotional dysregulation that DSM omits but clinicians see constantly.

"Delayed, not deviant" — the maturation lag

Shaw et al. (2007, PNAS) tracked cortical thickness longitudinally and found the ADHD cortex reached peak thickness at a median of ~10.5 years vs ~7.5 years in controls — a ~3-year delay, greatest in prefrontal regions governing attention and executive control. The shape of the developmental curve was normal; the timing was shifted. This is one of the most hopeful findings in the field: it reframes ADHD as a difference in developmental tempo.

CHAPTER 05
The physical brain

The real headline: networks that won't take turns.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Modern models focus on network dynamics, not just single regions.
  • The Default Mode Network (daydreaming) intrudes on focused work — the "Default Mode Interference Hypothesis."
  • Attention lapses can be predicted in the data seconds before they happen.

The brain runs several large-scale networks that should coordinate like an orchestra taking turns:

  • Default Mode Network (DMN): medial PFC, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus — active during rest, mind-wandering, self-reference.
  • Central Executive / Frontoparietal Network: switches on for goal-directed, effortful focus.
  • Salience Network (anterior insula, dorsal ACC): the switchboard that decides which of the above should be in charge right now.
  • Dorsal & ventral attention networks: orient and re-orient attention to relevant stimuli.

In neurotypical brains, DMN and executive networks are anti-correlated: when you focus outward, the daydreaming network quiets down. In ADHD, this reciprocity is weakened — the DMN keeps intruding during tasks. Sonuga-Barke and Castellanos formalised this as the Default Mode Interference Hypothesis: spontaneous low-frequency DMN activity "breaks through" into task-focused states, producing the periodic attention lapses that define the disorder. A faulty salience network that mis-assigns what deserves attention is increasingly implicated as the switch that fails.

🐿 Deep-dive

Resting-state fMRI shows both hypo- and hyper-connectivity patterns in ADHD depending on network and age. The picture isn't "less connected" but "differently orchestrated" — with weaker segregation between networks that should stay distinct. This is why focus feels effortful: you're manually holding networks apart that normally separate on their own.

CHAPTER 06
The chemistry

It's not "low dopamine." It's bad signal-to-noise.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Two catecholamines run the show: dopamine (drive/reward) and norepinephrine (focus/alertness).
  • The problem is weak, mistimed signalling — poor signal-to-noise — not simply "too little."
  • The prefrontal cortex needs a Goldilocks amount: too little or too much both wreck focus (the inverted-U).

The neurotransmitters involved

Primary · "the wanting molecule"

Dopamine (DA)

Codes motivation, anticipation, reward-prediction — not pleasure itself. Low, noisy DA signalling makes future rewards feel faint and boring tasks unbearable.

Primary · "the alertness dial"

Norepinephrine (NE)

Sets cortical arousal and signal-to-noise. Enough NE and the important signal stands out from background chatter.

Modulatory

Serotonin (5-HT)

Tunes impulsivity, mood, and emotional regulation. Implicated in the impulsive and emotional-dysregulation facets.

Excitatory / inhibitory balance

Glutamate & GABA

The brain's accelerator and brake. Altered glutamate/GABA balance in frontostriatal circuits is an active research frontier.

Emerging

Acetylcholine

Supports attention and cortical arousal; a target of some investigational compounds.

Reward-prediction error & delay discounting

Dopamine neurons fire not for reward itself but for surprise — the gap between expected and received reward (Schultz's reward-prediction error). In ADHD this signal is blunted and steeply time-discounted: a reward 20 minutes away barely registers while an immediate one screams. This delay aversion is the neural root of "I know it matters, I just can't make myself start." It also explains why ADHD is a disorder of performance (doing what you know) more than knowledge (knowing what to do).

🔬 Tonic vs phasic dopamine

Dopamine operates on two timescales: a steady tonic background level and sharp phasic bursts for salient events (Grace's model). ADHD is linked to dysregulation of this balance — a low, unstable tonic baseline against which phasic bursts are hard to read. The brain is straining to hear a whisper over static.

The dopamine transporter & how stimulants work

After dopamine acts, the dopamine transporter (DAT) reuptakes it. Many people with ADHD carry DAT/receptor variants that clear it too quickly. Stimulants correct this at the source:

  • Methylphenidate blocks the DAT and norepinephrine transporter (NET) — reuptake inhibition, leaving more transmitter in the synapse.
  • Amphetamines block reuptake and reverse the transporters to actively pump dopamine out, plus promote release from vesicles.

The net effect: the signal lasts long enough to be heard. This is why stimulants calm rather than excite someone with ADHD — they raise the under-aroused brain toward the optimal band.

The inverted-U (this one explains everything)

Amy Arnsten's work shows prefrontal performance follows an inverted-U against catecholamine levels. Too little → understimulated, distractible, bored. Too much (e.g. acute stress dumping NE/DA) → frazzled, overwhelmed, frozen. Optimal signalling engages postsynaptic α2A and D1 receptors in a narrow band. ADHD tends to sit on the left, under-aroused slope — which is why both understimulation and high stress tank focus.

The inverted-U (Arnsten). Focus lives in a narrow middle band; ADHD starts low, so the fix is nudging up toward the peak — not blasting past it.
CHAPTER 07
The cognitive model

Executive function: the theory that ties it together.

⚡ 30-second version
  • ADHD is best understood as impaired self-regulation / executive function.
  • Barkley's model puts response inhibition at the centre — everything else fails downstream.
  • It's a disorder of using what you know, at the point of performance — not of knowing.

Executive functions are the brain's self-management system — the mental tools that let you pursue goals over time. The core set:

Response inhibition

Stopping the automatic or impulsive response so a considered one can occur. Barkley's proposed keystone.

Working memory

Holding information "online" to act on it — including holding the future goal in mind against present temptation.

Emotional self-regulation

Modulating emotional reactions so they don't hijack behaviour — a core, under-recognised ADHD deficit.

Self-directed motivation

Generating drive for tasks with no immediate reward — the dopamine problem, felt from the inside.

Planning / problem-solving

Sequencing steps toward a goal and adjusting when things change.

Cognitive flexibility & sense of time

Shifting set when needed; perceiving and using time — "time blindness" is a hallmark.

Barkley's model: inhibition first

Russell Barkley argues ADHD is fundamentally a deficit of behavioural inhibition. Without the pause that inhibition creates, the other executive functions never get their window to operate. The consequence is a disorder of time and the future: behaviour is captured by the immediate context because the internally-represented future can't compete. His famous framing — ADHD is "not a disorder of knowing what to do, but of doing what you know" — is why pure information or willpower approaches fail, and why interventions must act at the point of performance.

Complementary models

  • Brown's model: ADHD as impaired coordination of six executive clusters (activation, focus, effort, emotion, memory, action) — emphasising it's a management problem, not an attention switch.
  • Dual-pathway model (Sonuga-Barke): two partly independent routes — a cognitive/executive deficit and a motivational delay-aversion deficit — explaining why not everyone with ADHD has the same profile.
  • Sluggish Cognitive Tempo / Cognitive Disengagement Syndrome: a proposed related-but-distinct profile of dreaminess, slow processing, and hypoactivity, overlapping with inattentive ADHD.
CHAPTER 08
The hormonal brain

Why symptoms rise and fall — the endocrine axes.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Cortisol (stress) impairs the prefrontal cortex — chronic stress amplifies every symptom.
  • Estrogen boosts dopamine, so symptoms swing with the menstrual cycle and spike in perimenopause.
  • A delayed body clock (late melatonin) drags sleep and next-day focus out of alignment.

Cortisol & the HPA axis — the focus-killer

Under stress, the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis releases cortisol alongside a norepinephrine surge. Acutely, this pushes you up and over the inverted-U into the frozen zone. Chronically, elevated cortisol impairs and remodels the prefrontal cortex (dendritic retraction) while strengthening the reactive amygdala — the precise opposite of what an ADHD brain needs. Many people with ADHD also show a blunted or dysregulated cortisol awakening response, part of why routine, novelty, and pressure affect them unusually.

Estrogen & the dopamine connection

Estrogen is a dopamine amplifier — it boosts synthesis, increases receptor density, and modulates the transporter. So dopamine-dependent functions ride the hormonal tide:

  • Menstrual cycle: focus and mood often dip in the late-luteal / premenstrual phase as estrogen falls; some experience a premenstrual worsening of ADHD symptoms.
  • Perimenopause: declining estrogen commonly unmasks or worsens ADHD — a major driver of late-in-life diagnosis in women.
  • Postpartum: the sharp estrogen drop after birth can trigger a symptom flare.
  • Puberty: rising and fluctuating sex hormones reshape symptom presentation in adolescence.

Melatonin & the shifted body clock

ADHD is strongly associated with delayed sleep phase: dim-light melatonin onset runs roughly 1–1.5 hours late, so the brain isn't "sleepy" at a conventional bedtime. Late sleep → short sleep → a next-day prefrontal cortex on fumes → worse symptoms → later revenge-scrolling. It's self-reinforcing, and one of the highest-leverage things to fix.

Thyroid, insulin & the supporting cast

Thyroid hormone

Sets metabolic tempo. Hypothyroidism mimics and worsens inattention; rare thyroid-resistance syndromes are strongly linked to ADHD-like symptoms. Always worth ruling out.

Insulin / blood glucose

Blood-sugar crashes trigger adrenaline and cortisol, mimicking and worsening irritability and inattention — the mechanism behind the "protein breakfast" cheatcode.

Testosterone

Prenatal androgen exposure is one hypothesis for the male skew; its role in adult symptoms is less clear.

Adrenaline (epinephrine)

The acute-stress partner to cortisol; drives the "deadline adrenaline" that paradoxically helps some people focus (a natural push up the inverted-U).

🐿 Why this matters historically

For most of the 20th century, ADHD research was done on hyperactive boys. The hormonal picture is a major reason the inattentive, internalising presentation — more common in girls and women — was missed for generations. The chemistry was on a schedule nobody was measuring.

CHAPTER 09
Across people & time

Sex differences & the lifespan trajectory.

⚡ 30-second version
  • ADHD looks different by sex and changes shape across the lifespan.
  • Girls/women are under-diagnosed because inattentive symptoms are quieter and often masked.
  • Hyperactivity fades; inattention and executive challenges persist into adulthood.

How presentation shifts with age

Life stageTypical presentation
PreschoolOvert hyperactivity, impulsivity, difficult temperament; hard to distinguish from normal exuberance.
School ageClassic combined picture; academic and behavioural problems surface as demands rise.
AdolescenceHyperactivity internalises to restlessness; risk-taking, emotional dysregulation, and executive demands peak.
AdulthoodInattention, disorganisation, procrastination, time-management and relationship difficulties dominate; overt hyperactivity often gone.
Older adulthoodUnder-researched; symptoms persist and can be masked by or confused with age-related cognitive change.

Why women are diagnosed decades late

  • Presentation: more inattentive, less disruptive — so less likely to be referred.
  • Masking & compensation: social pressure drives coping strategies that hide symptoms until demands (career, motherhood, perimenopause) overwhelm them.
  • Hormonal fluctuation (see Chapter 08) modulates symptom severity in ways male-derived diagnostic norms never captured.
  • Misdiagnosis as anxiety or depression, which are often secondary to unmanaged ADHD.

The strengths side

Rigorous framing matters: the traits are context-dependent, not uniformly negative. Under the right conditions, features associated with ADHD include hyperfocus on high-interest tasks, divergent/creative thinking, resilience, and high energy for novel challenges. The disorder is defined by impairment in a mismatched environment — which is exactly why environmental design (Chapters 11 & 14) is so powerful.

CHAPTER 11
The modern trap

Social media is a slot machine built for your weak spot.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Phones don't cause ADHD — but they're engineered to exploit its exact wiring.
  • Unpredictable rewards (the "maybe" of a refresh) are the most compulsive schedule known to science.
  • After each spike comes a dip below baseline — making real life feel flat by comparison.

An ADHD brain is hungry for salient, immediate, novel dopamine. A feed delivers exactly that, on the one schedule the brain can't resist:

1

Variable-ratio rewardThe next swipe might be amazing. Skinner proved unpredictable rewards drive far more compulsive behaviour than reliable ones — a slot machine in your pocket, aimed at an already trigger-happy reward system.

2

Supernormal stimuliFeeds are faster, more novel, and more intense than anything in nature. They raise your reward set-point, so a book, a spreadsheet, or a conversation feels under-stimulating by comparison.

3

The dopamine dipEvery spike is followed by a compensatory drop below baseline (the pleasure-pain balance). Chase enough spikes and your resting state becomes restlessness. The scroll doesn't cure the itch — it is the itch.

4

No stopping cuesInfinite scroll and autoplay strip out the natural "you're done" signal — and a prefrontal cortex already bad at stopping never gets the cue.

5

The sleep taxBright screens at night suppress already-late melatonin, pushing sleep later, degrading tomorrow's focus, driving more scrolling. The loop closes.

The research here is largely correlational: heavy digital-media use is associated with more ADHD-type symptoms in adolescents (e.g. Ra et al., JAMA 2018), and clearly worsens the phenotype, but it is not proven to create the disorder. The honest takeaway: the attention economy is fine-tuned to press exactly the buttons your brain struggles to un-press — which makes environmental design (Chapter 14) non-optional for you.

CHAPTER 12
Interventions

Pharmacology, in depth.

⚡ 30-second version
  • ADHD medications are among the most effective, best-evidenced treatments in psychiatry (effect sizes ~0.7–1.0 for stimulants).
  • Two families: stimulants (first-line) and non-stimulants.
  • They work by restoring dopamine/norepinephrine signalling toward the optimal band.

Stimulants — first-line

ClassExamplesMechanism
MethylphenidateRitalin, Concerta, Medikinet, EquasymBlocks DAT & NET (reuptake inhibition) → more DA/NE in synapse.
AmphetaminesAdderall, Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine), Elvanse, dexamfetamineReuptake block plus transporter reversal & increased release.

Stimulants come in immediate- and extended-release forms; lisdexamfetamine is a prodrug (inactive until metabolised), giving a smooth curve and lower abuse potential. Roughly 70–80% of people respond to a given stimulant, and many non-responders to one class respond to the other. Common side effects: reduced appetite, sleep disruption, transient increased heart rate/blood pressure, and (in children) a small, largely catch-up growth effect. Contrary to old fears, appropriate stimulant treatment lowers the long-term risk of substance use disorder.

Non-stimulants

DrugTypeMechanism & use
AtomoxetineNET inhibitorSelectively raises norepinephrine (and dopamine in PFC). Non-controlled; takes weeks to work. Good where stimulants aren't tolerated or SUD is a concern.
Guanfacine XRα2A-adrenergic agonistDirectly strengthens prefrontal α2A signalling (Arnsten's mechanism). Helps with impulsivity, emotional regulation, tics, sleep.
Clonidine XRα2-adrenergic agonistSimilar; more sedating; useful adjunct especially for sleep and hyperarousal.

The evidence & the honest framing

Medication is not "cheating" and not a moral question. It corrects a signalling problem the same way glasses correct a focusing problem. The landmark MTA study found well-managed medication superior to behavioural therapy alone for core symptoms, with combined treatment best for overall functioning. Medication doesn't teach skills — it opens the window in which skills (Chapters 13–14) can finally be learned. Any medication decision belongs with a qualified clinician; this chapter is education, not a prescription.

CHAPTER 13
Interventions

Behavioural & psychosocial treatment.

⚡ 30-second version
  • Skills and structure work best alongside (not instead of) medication for moderate-severe ADHD.
  • The gold standards: CBT for adults, behavioural parent training for children, coaching, and structure.
  • All share one principle: act at the point of performance, externally.

CBT adapted for ADHD

Targets the practical fallout — procrastination, time management, organisation, and the negative self-talk built up over years. Strong evidence in adults, especially combined with medication.

Behavioural parent training

First-line for young children. Teaches consistent routines, clear commands, and reward systems that externalise motivation and structure.

ADHD coaching

Ongoing, action-oriented support for building and maintaining systems — accountability that offloads executive load onto a relationship.

School / workplace accommodations

Environmental redesign: extended time, quiet spaces, written instructions, deadline scaffolding — changing the environment, not the person.

Psychoeducation

Understanding the mechanism (this whole document) reduces shame and enables self-advocacy — a genuine active ingredient.

Emerging & adjunct

Mindfulness training, some digital therapeutics (e.g. FDA-cleared game-based tools), and exercise programmes as evidence-supported adjuncts.

🐿 What doesn't hold up

Elimination/restriction diets show only small effects in specific subgroups; "brain-training" apps rarely transfer beyond the trained task; neurofeedback has mixed, mostly modest evidence. Be skeptical of anything marketed as a drug-free "cure." The robust levers are medication, behavioural skills, sleep, and exercise.

CHAPTER 15
Interventions

Targeted fixes: a cheatcode for every symptom.

Chapter 14 covered the broad levers. This is the surgical version — one specific negative at a time, with the mechanism that causes it and the interventions that actually move it.

⚡ The meta-principle
  • Every fix below follows one rule: act at the point of performance, externally.
  • If a strategy requires the broken circuit to fix the broken circuit, it will fail. Put the solution in the world, not in your head.
✕ The negative

Task initiation failure — "the wall of awful"

You know exactly what to do and physically cannot start. Why: starting a low-reward task requires the mesocortical dopamine signal to bridge the gap to a delayed payoff — and that signal is blunted and steeply time-discounted. It's a motivation-circuit failure, not a knowledge failure.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Implementation intentions. "If it's 9am, then I open the doc and write one sentence." Pre-deciding the trigger converts effortful initiation into automatic cue→response. Among the largest effect sizes in behavioural science.
  • ▸Shrink the first step to absurdity. Not "write the report" — "open the file." Effort is discounted nonlinearly, so a trivially small step falls below the activation threshold.
  • ▸The 5-minute rule + Zeigarnik effect. Commit to 5 minutes only. Started-but-unfinished tasks generate intrusive tension that pulls you back — you'll usually continue.
  • ▸Body-doubling. Work alongside someone (in person or on a call). Social presence supplies external accountability and arousal the internal system won't.
  • ▸Temptation bundling. Pair the task with something you want (a specific playlist, a good coffee) available only during it.
Why it works: each one manufactures an immediate, proximal reward or removes the need for the initiation signal entirely.
✕ The negative

Time blindness

Time is either "now" or "not now." Hours vanish; deadlines arrive from nowhere. Why: impaired internal time representation, linked to cerebellar timing circuits and the working-memory deficit that makes the future hard to hold and therefore hard to feel.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Make time physically visible. An analog countdown timer that shows the shrinking block (Time Timer-style) converts an abstract quantity into a perceptual one your brain can actually track.
  • ▸Time-estimation logging. Guess how long a task will take, then record the real number. Weeks of this calibrates a genuinely miscalibrated internal model.
  • ▸Alarms as external anchors. Not one alarm at the deadline — alarms at the transitions. The deadline isn't your problem; the invisible runway before it is.
  • ▸Shrink the horizon. Break multi-week projects into daily-visible units. If the future can't be felt, only build plans that live inside today.
Barkley's principle: you cannot fix a broken internal clock — you can only put a working one outside your body.
✕ The negative

Working-memory failure & forgetfulness

You walk into a room and lose the reason. The thought evaporates mid-sentence. Why: working memory depends on sustained prefrontal firing held by D1/α2A receptor signalling — precisely the system that's under-tuned. Under-supported PFC networks can't hold a representation online against interference.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸One capture inbox. A single, always-available place for every thought (one app, one notebook — not five). Capture at the instant of thought; the thought will not survive the walk to the desk.
  • ▸Checklists. Aviation and surgery both cut catastrophic error with them precisely because expert memory is unreliable under load. Yours is unreliable at baseline.
  • ▸Single-task ruthlessly. Every concurrent stream consumes the same limited buffer. Multitasking isn't inefficient for you — it's destructive.
  • ▸Say it out loud. Verbalising recruits the phonological loop, a separate buffer from the visuospatial one — free extra capacity.
✕ The negative

Emotional dysregulation & rejection sensitivity (RSD)

Emotions arrive at full volume with no dimmer switch; mild criticism lands like catastrophe. Why: weak prefrontal top-down control over a reactive amygdala. The emotion isn't bigger — the brake is weaker. DSM omits this; clinicians see it constantly.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Affect labelling — "name it to tame it." Putting the feeling into words measurably reduces amygdala activity and recruits ventrolateral PFC (Lieberman et al.). Literally: say "this is rejection sensitivity firing."
  • ▸Cognitive reappraisal. Reframing the meaning of an event before the response is one of the best-validated regulation strategies in psychology (Gross).
  • ▸The physiological sigh. Two inhales through the nose, one long exhale. Extended exhales raise vagal tone and cut arousal faster than most techniques (Balban et al., 2023).
  • ▸The 24-hour send rule. Never respond to a perceived slight the same day. A hard external rule outperforms in-the-moment judgment — because in the moment, judgment is the thing that's offline.
  • ▸Ask about guanfacine. The α2A agonist directly strengthens the prefrontal signalling that regulates emotion — often more helpful for this facet than stimulants. Clinician conversation.
Note: "RSD" is a useful clinical description, not a formal DSM diagnosis. The underlying emotional dysregulation is very real and well documented.
✕ The negative

Impulsivity — blurting, spending, quitting, sending

The action happens before the decision does. Why: deficient response inhibition — Barkley's proposed keystone. Without the pause inhibition creates, no other executive function gets its window to run.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Pre-commitment devices. Bind your future self in advance: delete saved cards, use a separate account for spending money, put the drafts in a folder that sends at 8am. Ulysses had himself tied to the mast — he didn't rely on resolve.
  • ▸Inhibitory if-thens. "If I want to buy something over ₹5,000, then it goes on a list for 48 hours." Implementation intentions work for stopping, not just starting.
  • ▸Insert physical friction. Every second between impulse and action is a second the slow system might arrive. Logged out, another room, a different device.
  • ▸Occupy the channel. In meetings, write the thought down instead of saying it. The urge to blurt is discharged by the writing; the idea survives for the right moment.
✕ The negative

Boredom intolerance & understimulation

Low-stimulation tasks feel physically painful — not merely dull. Why: you sit on the left, under-aroused slope of the inverted-U. A task that generates no dopamine leaves the PFC below its operating threshold. Boredom is a genuine physiological state for you, not a character flaw.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Add noise — literally. The Moderate Brain Arousal model (Söderlund et al.) found white noise improves performance in ADHD while degrading it in controls — stochastic resonance: added noise lifts a weak signal over threshold. This is one of the most counterintuitive, best-mechanised findings in the field.
  • ▸Rotate the environment. New café, new room, new desk. Novelty is a phasic dopamine trigger — and it's free.
  • ▸Manufacture urgency. A visible 25-minute countdown creates a norepinephrine deadline-buzz that raises arousal into the usable band.
  • ▸Gamify the metric. Compete against your own last score. Turning an unrewarded task into a scored one gives the reward system something to predict.
✕ The negative

Physical restlessness & fidgeting

You can't sit still, and being forced to makes thinking worse. Why: here's the twist — Rapport and Sarver's work found that gross motor activity in ADHD increases specifically during working-memory demand and correlates with better performance. The fidgeting isn't the symptom interfering with the work. It's compensatory self-arousal — the brain manufacturing the stimulation it needs to think.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Stop suppressing it. Forcing stillness spends executive resources on sitting still — the exact resource you needed for the task. Let the leg bounce.
  • ▸Build movement into the work. Standing desk, walking meetings, pacing while on calls, reading while on a treadmill.
  • ▸Exercise before focus blocks. Front-load the arousal so the fidgeting has less work to do (see Cheatcode 01).
Reframe: a century of "sit still and concentrate" had the causality backwards for this population.
✕ The negative

Hyperfocus & the inability to switch

Six hours gone, meals skipped, the actual priority untouched. Why: the flip side of poor attentional control — the salience network fails to switch you out just as it fails to switch you in. Attention isn't deficient; it's badly governed in both directions.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸External interrupts you can't dismiss. An alarm across the room, a calendar block with a hard stop, a person expecting you. Internal "I'll stop at 6" has no force.
  • ▸Transition rituals. A fixed short sequence (stand, water, three breaths) that acts as an external switch signal between contexts.
  • ▸Aim it, don't kill it. Hyperfocus is a genuine asset when pointed at the right target. The intervention is selection, not suppression — decide before you start what deserves it.
✕ The negative

Disorganisation & "object permanence" loss

Out of sight is genuinely out of mind — the food rots in the opaque container; the file in the folder ceases to exist. Why: a working-memory system that can't hold unseen items online. If it isn't perceptually present, it isn't represented.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Transparent & open storage. Clear containers, open shelves, pegboards. Design for a brain that only knows what it can see.
  • ▸Own fewer things. Organisation load scales with object count. Reduction beats any system.
  • ▸One home per object, zero decisions. Keys live in exactly one place, forever. The goal is removing the micro-decision, not being tidy.
  • ▸Launch pad. One spot by the door holding everything that leaves with you. Externalises the entire morning checklist into a location.
✕ The negative

Overwhelm, shutdown & decision paralysis

Too many options or too big a pile, and the system freezes entirely. Why: a limited executive buffer flooded past capacity, plus the stress response pushing you over the right side of the inverted-U into the frozen zone. Shutdown is an overload state, not laziness.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Brain dump first, always. Everything onto paper before deciding anything. You cannot sort a list you're simultaneously trying to hold.
  • ▸Reduce to the next physical action. Not "sort out taxes" but "find the folder." GTD's core insight is an executive-function prosthetic.
  • ▸Satisfice, don't maximise. Schwartz's research: maximisers make marginally better choices and are reliably more miserable and slower. Pick the first option that clears the bar.
  • ▸Pre-set defaults. Same breakfast, same gym days, a uniform. Every decision you delete is executive budget returned to the work that matters.
✕ The negative

Revenge bedtime procrastination

2am, exhausted, still scrolling — reclaiming the only unclaimed hours of the day. Why: a double hit. Delayed melatonin (Ch. 08) means you're not biologically sleepy yet, and depleted daytime inhibition means the impulse to keep the "me time" wins. Then the sleep debt degrades tomorrow's PFC and the loop tightens.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Attack the wake end, not bedtime. Fixed wake time + morning light pulls the whole delayed clock earlier. Willing yourself to sleep earlier fails; re-timing the clock works.
  • ▸Schedule the "me time" in daylight. The behaviour is compensatory — if the day contains no unclaimed hours, the night will take them. Fix the cause.
  • ▸Wind-down alarm, not a bedtime. An alarm at the start of the wind-down, because you will not notice time passing (see: time blindness).
  • ▸Charge the phone in another room. The single highest-yield friction intervention available.
CHAPTER 16
Interventions

Targeted fixes: the comorbidities.

Chapter 10 listed what travels with ADHD. This is what to actually do about each one — including the sequencing question, which is usually the whole ballgame.

⚡ The sequencing rule
  • Much of ADHD's comorbidity is secondary — anxiety and depression built on years of unexplained underperformance.
  • Treating the ADHD often shrinks the comorbidity. Treating only the anxiety while ADHD runs unmanaged tends to fail.
  • The exception: severe depression or active substance use is stabilised first. This ordering is a clinician's call, not a self-diagnosis.
✕ Comorbidity · ~25–40%

Anxiety disorders

The relationship: often secondary — a lifetime of missed deadlines and unpredictable performance is a rational anxiety generator. Anxiety also mimics ADHD (both fragment attention), and it can mask it: anxiety-driven hypervigilance can look like functioning right up until it collapses.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Treat the ADHD and re-measure. When the chaos generating the anxiety subsides, a lot of the anxiety goes with it. Assess after, not before.
  • ▸CBT. Gold standard for anxiety, and it stacks well with ADHD-adapted CBT.
  • ▸Exercise. One of the most robust non-drug anxiolytics known — and it's already Cheatcode 01. A rare two-for-one.
  • ▸Know the medication nuance. Stimulants can worsen anxiety in some people — guanfacine or atomoxetine are common alternatives when they do, since they raise focus without the same jitter. Clinician territory.
Mechanism note: anxiety pushes you up the right side of the inverted-U. If you're anxious and ADHD, you can be over-aroused and under-focused simultaneously — which is why "just calm down" and "just try harder" both fail.
✕ Comorbidity · ~20–30%

Depression & demoralisation

The relationship: critical distinction — demoralisation (a reasonable response to repeated failure, lifts when function improves) vs a primary mood disorder (autonomous, needs its own treatment). ADHD also carries genuinely elevated MDD risk. Both share dopaminergic reward-circuit involvement.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Behavioural activation. Act first, motivation second — schedule rewarding activity rather than waiting to feel like it. Strong evidence, and it's mechanistically perfect for ADHD, which also can't wait for motivation.
  • ▸Exercise. Comparable to antidepressants for mild-moderate depression in several meta-analyses, with the ADHD benefits stacked on top.
  • ▸Psychoeducation as an intervention. Reframing 30 years of "lazy" as a dopamine-signalling difference is not a consolation prize — shame reduction measurably shifts mood and self-efficacy.
  • ▸Get the sequencing right. Severe depression is stabilised first — a depressed brain can't build ADHD systems. Mild demoralisation often lifts once ADHD is treated.
✕ Comorbidity · the majority

Insomnia & delayed sleep phase

The relationship: bidirectional and vicious. ADHD delays the clock (late melatonin onset); the resulting sleep loss degrades the PFC, which worsens ADHD, which delays sleep further. Sleep deprivation alone produces an ADHD-like phenotype in anyone — so untreated sleep problems are actively inflating your symptoms.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸CBT-I. Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia is the gold-standard first-line treatment — more effective than sleeping pills, and durable. Widely available as structured programmes.
  • ▸Low-dose timed melatonin. Not a sedative — a clock signal. Small doses (~0.5–1mg) taken several hours before target sleep shift the phase; large doses at bedtime mostly don't. RCT evidence specifically in ADHD (Van der Heijden et al.). Discuss with a clinician.
  • ▸Morning light + fixed wake time. The other half of the phase-shift lever (Cheatcodes 02–03).
  • ▸Screen for the other culprits. Restless legs and sleep apnoea are both over-represented and both fully treatable. Worth ruling out before assuming it's "just ADHD."
✕ Comorbidity · elevated risk

Substance use

The relationship: substantially elevated risk, and much of it is self-medication — nicotine, caffeine, cocaine and alcohol all touch the catecholamine system an ADHD brain is short on. Add impulsivity and reward-seeking and the risk compounds.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Treat the ADHD — it lowers the risk. This reverses the intuition and it's important: large registry studies (Chang et al.) find appropriate stimulant treatment is associated with reduced subsequent substance-use risk. Untreated ADHD is the risk factor.
  • ▸Use abuse-deterrent formulations. Prodrugs (lisdexamfetamine) and long-acting forms have smooth curves and much lower misuse liability than immediate-release.
  • ▸Non-stimulant options exist. Atomoxetine and guanfacine aren't controlled substances — a genuine path when misuse is a live concern.
  • ▸Integrated treatment. Treating SUD and ADHD together beats treating either alone; active severe SUD is usually stabilised first.
✕ Comorbidity · strongly linked

Binge eating & disordered eating

The relationship: ADHD is strongly associated with binge-eating disorder — same impulsivity plus the same reward-deficiency driving food-seeking for stimulation. Interoception is often poor too: hunger goes unnoticed until it's desperate, and stimulants suppress appetite, producing a skip-then-binge pattern.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Know the pharmacology overlap. Lisdexamfetamine is FDA-approved for binge-eating disorder — the same drug, the same impulse-control mechanism, two labels. A striking confirmation the circuits are shared.
  • ▸Eat on a schedule, not on hunger. If interoception is unreliable, don't route decisions through it. Timed meals prevent the deficit that triggers the binge.
  • ▸Protect breakfast on stimulants. Appetite suppression peaks mid-day — eat before the dose lands.
  • ▸Environment over willpower, again. Binge foods not in the house is a stronger intervention than any resolve at 11pm.
✕ Comorbidity · ~40–50% of children

Oppositional defiant & conduct problems

The relationship: very common alongside childhood combined-type ADHD, and heavily shaped by the coercive cycle — an impulsive child and an exhausted parent train each other into escalation.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Behavioural parent training. First-line, strongest evidence for this combination. It changes the environment and the interaction pattern rather than trying to change the child directly.
  • ▸Treat the underlying ADHD. Much oppositionality is impulsivity plus frustration plus demand overload. Reduce those and the defiance often drops with them.
  • ▸Immediate, frequent, specific consequences. Delayed rewards don't reach an ADHD brain (delay discounting again) — the reinforcement must be far more immediate than it would be for a neurotypical child.
✕ Comorbidity · ~20–40%

Specific learning disorders (dyslexia, dyscalculia)

The relationship: distinct conditions that frequently co-travel. Crucially — ADHD medication does not treat dyslexia. It may let a child attend to reading instruction, but it doesn't build the phonological skill. Conflating the two wastes years.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Assess separately, treat separately. Two problems, two interventions. Persistent reading failure on well-managed ADHD means the reading disorder was never addressed.
  • ▸Structured literacy instruction. Explicit, systematic phonics-based remediation — the evidence base here is deep and unambiguous.
  • ▸Assistive tech as a bypass. Text-to-speech, speech-to-text, audiobooks. Remediate the skill and route around it so learning doesn't stall meanwhile.
✕ Comorbidity · ~20–50%

Co-occurring autism (AuDHD)

The relationship: shared genetic architecture, and dual diagnosis has only been permitted since DSM-5 (2013) — so a lot of people were forced into one box. The combination creates genuine internal conflict: an autistic need for routine and sameness against an ADHD need for novelty and stimulation.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Design for the sensory profile first. Sensory overload consumes the same executive budget you need for everything else. Noise-cancelling headphones, lighting control, and predictable environments are load-reduction, not indulgence.
  • ▸Routine as the frame, novelty inside it. Resolve the conflict structurally: keep the scaffolding fixed and predictable, vary the content within it.
  • ▸Expect medication nuance. Response can differ and side-effect sensitivity is often higher — "start low, go slow" is the usual clinical posture.
✕ Near-universal, rarely coded

Shame, low self-esteem & the "lazy" narrative

The relationship: not in any diagnostic manual, and arguably the most damaging thing ADHD carries. Children with ADHD receive an estimated 20,000 more corrective or negative messages by age 12 than their peers. That's not a side-effect — it's a formative input.
✓ The cheatcodes
  • ▸Understand the mechanism. This entire document is the intervention. "My dopamine signalling makes delayed rewards faint" is actionable; "I'm lazy" is not.
  • ▸Self-compassion, specifically. Neff's research finds it outperforms self-esteem work for resilience — and unlike self-esteem, it doesn't require you to be succeeding first.
  • ▸ADHD-adapted CBT. Explicitly targets the accumulated negative self-talk alongside the practical systems. Strong adult evidence.
  • ▸Community. Peer contact with other ADHD adults reliably reduces the isolation of thinking you're uniquely broken.
The reframe: you have failed at neurotypical strategies. That is exactly what should have happened. The strategies were built for different hardware.
CHAPTER 17
Set the record straight

Myths that aren't true.

✕ Myth

"ADHD is just a lack of willpower / discipline."

✓ Fact

It's a measurable difference in brain development, network switching, and catecholamine signalling. Willpower runs on the exact circuit that's under-powered.

✕ Myth

"You can't have ADHD — you focus for hours on games/art."

✓ Fact

ADHD is dysregulated attention, not absent attention. High-dopamine, novel tasks flood the weak signal — that's why hyperfocus coexists with distractibility.

✕ Myth

"Sugar and screens cause ADHD."

✓ Fact

ADHD is ~74–80% heritable. Sugar doesn't cause it; screens don't cause it — but the modern attention economy clearly worsens the phenotype.

✕ Myth

"Kids grow out of it."

✓ Fact

Hyperactivity often fades; the executive-function and attention differences frequently persist into adulthood in a quieter disguise.

✕ Myth

"Stimulant meds are just legal speed / change your personality."

✓ Fact

At therapeutic doses they normalise a quiet signalling channel. Done right, people describe feeling more themselves, not less — and treatment lowers later substance-use risk.

✕ Myth

"It's over-diagnosed and basically made up."

✓ Fact

Prevalence is stable across cultures; it's one of the most validated, most heritable conditions in psychiatry. Under-diagnosis (esp. girls, women, adults) is the bigger real-world problem.

The fine print

References & honest caveats

Everything here is an average-level, statistical picture. ADHD is deeply heterogeneous — no two brains, no two people, present the same. Mechanisms are simplified for clarity; the science is genuinely still moving.

⚠️ This is education, not medical advice. Nothing here diagnoses, treats, or replaces a qualified clinician. If ADHD is affecting your life, seek a proper assessment — especially before starting, stopping, or changing any medication or supplement.
